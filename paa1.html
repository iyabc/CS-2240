The How, Where, When, and Why

Alan Mathison Turing

Born on June 23, 1912 in Paddington, London, Alan is the the youngest of the two children of Julius Mathison Turing and Ethel Sara Turing. His family belonged to the upper-middle-class, with his father working for the Indian Civil Service (ICS) and his mother as the daughter of the engineer of the Madras Railway.

Alan's mother went to India to reunite with his father when he was about a year old, leaving Alan under the supervision of family friends. He went to St. Michael's in 1918, Hazel Hurst Preparatory School in 1922, and Sherborne School in 1926. He showed a great interest in chess and eventually joined the debating society.

UNIVERSITY

King's College

In 1931, Turing studied mathematics in King's College in London, United Kingdom. He graduated with first class honors in 1934, and was elected as a Fellow of the college in 1935.

Princeton University

Turing earned his Ph.D. in mathematical logic after studying in the university from 1936 to 1938.

WARTIME SERVICE

Bletchley Park

A day after the declaration of the WWII in 1939, Turing arrived at the Bletchley Park after being requested to join the Government Codes and Ciphers School where he took up a full-time role in deciphering German military codes. This lead to the development of the Bombe in 1940.

The Enigma Code

With the help of the Bombe, Turing and his team were able to decrypt German secret messages encrypted by the Enigma in 1942. Polish mathematicians lead by Marian Rejewski were the first to read Enigma messages before the Germans increased its security, which lead to them turning over the messages to the British.

Post-War

Order of the British Empire

A year after the end of WWII in 1945, Turing was awarded an OBE for his achievements and contributions in which he helped in the decyphering of German codes that helped change the course of the war.

Published Works

In 1946, Turing joined the National Physical Laboratory where he published a paper with the first the first detailed design of a stored-program computer. He also published a report called 'Intelligent machinery' that discusses about the possiblity of machines to possess the intelligence of a human. In 1950, he published'Computing Machinery and Intelligence' in which he develops the Turing Test, an attempt to define a standard for a machine to be called intelligent.

In 1952, Turing started a relationship with a 19-year-old man named Alan Murray. Murray then burgled Turing's house, then reported the incident to the police. This caused Turing's acknowledgement of his homosexual relationship with Murray, which lead to his prosecution for his homosexual acts. He opted for chemical castration by a series of injections of female hormones as an alternative to being imprisoned.

DEATH

The How, Where, When, and Why

On June 7, 1954, 16 days before his 42nd birthday, Turing died in his home in Wilmslow, Chesire, England. He had died a day earlier and was found the next by his housekeeper. The cause of death was determined to be suicide by cyanide poisoning. A half-eaten apple laced was cyanide was found next to his body. There are many speculations as to why Turing did it, many of which are associated with the mental effects of the medication inflicted on to him by the court and the discriminations thrown unto him due to his sexuality. In 2013, Turing received a posthumous royal pardon granted by Queen Elizabeth II, almost 60 years after his conviction. He is considered the "Father of Modern Computer Science".
